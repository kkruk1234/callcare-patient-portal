S:
Patient reports tick bite. Reports a tick bite with a bullseye rash present. No systemic symptoms reported. Exposure occurred in Georgia.
O: Unable to perform physical exam due to phone encounter. Patient describes characteristic erythema migrans rash (bullseye appearance).

A:
  - Working diagnosis (most likely): Early localized Lyme disease
  - Differential:
    1. Early Lyme disease
    2. Southern tick-associated rash illness (STARI)
    3. Cellulitis or other tick-bite associated skin infection
    4. Allergic reaction to tick bite
  - Red flags considered: No systemic symptoms such as fever, chills, malaise, or neurologic symptoms reported. No signs of severe allergic reaction or anaphylaxis.

P:
  - Disposition: Routine follow-up with primary care provider recommended
  - Plan: Initiate empiric antibiotic treatment for early Lyme disease as per guidelines (e.g., doxycycline 100 mg twice daily for 10-21 days), given presence of erythema migrans and tick exposure >36 hours in an endemic area. Educate patient on signs of progression or systemic symptoms. No immediate emergency care required.
  - Safety net: Advise patient to seek urgent care if fever, worsening rash, joint pain, neurologic symptoms, or other systemic signs develop. Return for in-person evaluation if rash expands or new symptoms arise.

Evidence (snippets used): CDC guidance recommends treatment of erythema migrans with doxycycline when tick exposure >36 hours in endemic areas such as Georgia. Early Lyme disease diagnosis is clinical based on characteristic rash and exposure history.
